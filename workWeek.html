In 1926, Henry Ford introduced the five-day, 40-hour workweek for his employees at the Ford Motor Company, revolutionizing the industrial workplace. His decision was motivated by a desire to improve worker productivity and morale, believing that shorter work hours would lead to better output.

Ford also recognized that giving workers more free time would allow them to enjoy the products they helped produce, like cars, contributing to greater consumer demand.

From then, people had believed that hours would continue to go down, perhaps even having the week be shortened by another day, yet we still have in fact gone up from the 8 hour day, to nearly 9 hours.

Studies show that longer work hours actully lead to the opposite of what bosses imagine them to; rather then being able to focus on nothing but the product becoming more efficient, these people are more likely to experience cognitive decline.

There are even studies that show that longer work hours often lead to decreased productivity. Research from Stanford University found that after 50 hours a week, productivity per hour drops significantly, and after 55 hours, total output declines. Workers become fatigued and lose focus, leading to inefficiency and wasted time.
